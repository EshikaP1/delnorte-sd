Overview:

Biofilm is made when the bacterial cells adhere to a surface and produce a matrix of Extracellular Polymeric Substance (EPS). They become embedded in this matrix and it protects them from being destroyed. Therefore, before destroying any bacteria, the extracellular matrix around is necessary to be destroyed first. This biofilm is mainly made up of mono and polysaccharides. In Cutibacterium Acne, major components that make up the biofilm include proteins, lipids, and sugars, which all help to maintain the structural integrity of the biofilm. By using enzyme inhibition/substrate analog inhibition/gene editing in targeting the N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphate-uridyltransferase/glucosamine-1-phosphate-acetyltransferase (GlmU), can be prevented and disrupt biofilm structure, which then can allow reach into destroying the bacteria that causes the acne that lays inside it.

Problem Statement: Cutibacterium acnes is one of the most common forms of acne, affecting over 45 million individuals in the United States alone, particularly adolescents. Cutibacterium can implicate conditions such as endocarditis, endophthalmitis, septic arthritis, and more. Furthermore, acne vulgaris causes an immense amount of psychological distress, such as reduced mental health and self-esteem. This can further lead to the suffering of adolescents all across the United States. The Cutibacterium creates a protective biofilm on the skin, hindering the effectiveness of topical treatments for acne vulgaris. Destroying this biofilm is necessary for enhancing the value of acne treatments, further leading to improved skin appearance and overall acne management.

Synthetic Biology Application: This project utilizes synthetic biology to eradicate biofilms by targeting their polysaccharide matrix. Our project does this by targeting the N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphate-uridyltransferase/glucosamine-1-phosphate-acetyltransferase (GlmU) enzyme found in Cutibacterium Acne. This can disrupt the biofilm structure which allows the destruction of the bacteria that causes acne. By using enzyme inhibitors, substrate analogs like UDP-2-deoxy-2-fluoro-N-acetylglucosamine or CRISPR-Cas9 to knock out genes encoding these enzymes, we aim to disrupt biofilm formation. This approach will expose the bacteria, allowing for their destruction and offering a potential treatment strategy for conditions like acne.

Part of the pathway for acetamido sugar biosynthesis in bacteria and archaea. The enzyme from several bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis and Haemophilus influenzae) has been shown to be bifunctional and also to possess the activity of EC 2.3.1.157, glucosamine-1-phosphate N-acetyltransferase. The enzyme from plants and animals is also active toward N-acetyl-alpha-D-galactosamine 1-phosphate (cf. EC 2.7.7.83, UDP-N-acetylgalactosamine diphosphorylase), while the bacterial enzyme shows low activity toward that substrate.
